SEIZE THE DATA

BREAKDOWN

Industry: MediaSmarts — Canada's Centre for Digital & Media Literacy

Strategies: User Testing, Wireframing, Multi-disciplinary Management

Task: Create an educational game for children about internet privacy.

Problem: Children give out personal information on the internet without understanding the repercussions. They aren’t aware of the tactics online predators have, which makes children easy targets.

Solution: Create a Match-3 game with a basic, interactive storyline to teach children about keeping their information safe.

My Roles: Game Designer & Developer, Storyteller, Audio Producer

Group Members: Abbie Goulet, Anjelica Maglinao, Amina Musa, Ethan Pelletier, Celine (Xiaolin) Qiu

WHY MATCH-3?

In January 2016, MediaSmarts approached our University program, GBDA, about teaching media literacy to children through video games. My group was assigned to teach about “online information privacy".

I did research into children’s games to determine our direction. This research concluded that children:

  • Appreciate simplicity — linear games, basic puzzles
  • Enjoy colours — items, effects, and a bright colour scheme
  • Connect with characters that talk directly to them
  • Learn by doing, not reading instructions
  • Get addicted easily — replayability, collectable items, completion rewards

Based on this information, I brainstormed potential ideas for the game. I’ve previously programmed games using Unity and Java, however this project was to be done in Javascript, so I didn’t want to make the game too complex for myself.

I chose Match-3 as the base framework because it fit all the above criteria, and many Match-3 projects existed online as open source code. I could simply edit and add to this code instead of “re-inventing the wheel”.

THE STORY

Successful children’s games tend to have mascots, and ours was Business Boy — a friendly, relatable character with role-model characteristics. He walked players through the tutorial and propelled the plot.

Players “worked” for Business Boy, collecting the data of other people online by matching icons of 3 or more. These icons mimicked pieces of information: name, gender, birthday, location, and credit card number. Players needed to unlock enough information before running out of moves.

Throughout the game, prompts appeared on-screen, requesting information like name, gender, etc. of the player in exchange for more moves. At the game’s end, Business Boy used this information to reveal how much he knew about the player, followed by a short list of helpful privacy practices.

FINAL RESULT

After user testing, tweaks were made and features were added. The project had a tight deadline — 3 months from inception to completion, so not every idea could make it to the final game. My team and I presented the finished product to MediaSmarts in April 2016 to very positive feedback.

If I revisited the game in the future to enhance it, I would:

  • Create multiple levels with collectible character cards
  • Separate different privacy topics into their own levels
  • Deepen the plot to make the final message more powerful
  • Add unlockable levels, achievements, and social sharing

This project pushed my abilities to learn new programming languages in a pinch, and allowed me to coordinate with graphic designers to manage my section of the project.
