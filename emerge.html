EMERGE

BREAKDOWN

Industry: Jane Tingley & The City of Stratford, ON

Strategies: Agile Design Sprints, User Testing, A/B Testing

Task: Design an art installation to improve the waiting room experience.

Problem: Emergency waiting rooms are the darkest, gloomiest parts of the hospital, and are often the most travelled. The atmosphere creates a feeling of unease, stress, and worry.

Solution: Distract visitors in the waiting room with an unobtrusive, relaxing, digital landscape controllable by the visitors.

My Roles: UX Researcher, UX Designer, Developer

Group Members: Mara Finkelstein, Marissa Harley, Anjelica Maglinao

UX RESEARCH

My group travelled to different hospitals, researching their waiting rooms to determine what could be improved about them. Considering the sensitivity of hospital-goers, we chose not to interview any visitors.

Most waiting rooms tended to be open-concept, with lots of natural light, a muted colour pallette, and art hanging on the walls. A TV was always on, and there were occassinally magazines on side tables.

In contrast, all emergency rooms we visited were dark and gray, with posters about various diseases hanging on the walls. The TVs only displayed hospital news and weather, and the space felt compact.

Our goal was instantly clear — create an art installation that:

  • Distracted users from their surroundings
  • Was subtle, calming, and colourful
  • Opened the room visually beyond its walls
  • Adapted to any emergency room size
Regular Waiting Room
Emergency Waiting Room

We asked hospital employees and owners why emergency rooms tended to be dark and gloomy.

Emergency rooms are the first parts of the hospital built, so they naturally feel older and out-of-date. They also must minimize on anything that could create sensory overload (e.g. bright colours, repetitive noises, odours).

USER TESTING

From chairs to side tables, projections to floors, all aspects of the emergency room were considered when creating the interactive experience. It could not take up additional space unless it was very small, so it needed to be a part of existing architecture.

We discovered people like to look at “the ideal landscape”. This is a human-generated landscape of a path cutting through hills to plains and mountains on the horizon, with a prominent object in the foreground. The idea is to capture as much depth as possible in one image.

Ideal Landscape

Our idea: Activating various pressure sensors on a chair would adjust an ideal landscape on the room’s TV. These changes could be a fish jumping in a pond, or seasons changing. Our first round of testing determined if it was better to have labelled or unlabelled sensors.

When users hit implicit or explicit “sensor points”, I would react by performing a certain action. We tested to determine which types of sensors users preferred.

Testing concluded that people had more fun with unlabelled sensors — figuring out what triggered an action boosted a sense of discovery. However, having too many different sensors made the experience loud and unpleasant.

The product was meant foremost to be casual and easy, not adventurous.

Our second user test involved a product prototype closely mimicking the actual visitor experience. Our ideal landscape had yet to be completed, so I programmed a mock scene featuring a potted plant. When users interacted with their chair, I would trigger corresponding commands to make the colours change and the petals move.

When users sat down, I would reveal the on-screen flower. Leaning on different parts of the armchair triggered leaves changing colour and petals rotating.

Testing concluded that multiple sensors made the installation too busy. Users liked being able to manipulate the image, but thought if lots of people were moving in their chairs to play with the image, it would create a significant disturbance in the room.

We put our user testing data into action by doing the following:

  • Simplifying each chair to have an on/off state
  • Activating this state by sitting down in a chair
  • Adapting to pre-set room sizes of 10, 25, and 50 chairs
  • Creating an ideal landscape as a base image, designing each chair to add new details to the scene (e.g. rocks, clouds, trees)

THE FINAL PRODUCT

The name Emerge was inspired by the product being made for emergency rooms, and how elements of the scene appeared and disappeared as though emerging from the background.

Emerge used cost-effective software and hardware for the product, keeping production costs low. This way, any hospital could afford to buy the product.

Emerge would come as a pre-programmed set of sensors with an HDMI-USB connector (similar to Chromecast technology), so hospitals could purchase and set up the whole interaction themselves with reasonable ease. The product could scale for multiple room sizes.
