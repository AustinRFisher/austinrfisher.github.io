UX INTERNSHIP

BREAKDOWN

Industry: University of Waterloo — Institutional Analysis & Planning (IAP)

My Roles: UX Tester, Copy Editor, Document & Graphic Designer

Tools Used: Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator, & InDesign, WCMS

WRITING & FORMATTING

I was hired as a Web and Print Content Creator to solve critical issues for the University. One project spanned the entire 4 month internship — The Strategic Plan. I developed the whole document by:

  • Formatting and designing 40+ unique pages
  • Visualizing data to complement descriptions
  • Re-colouring and editing supplied images
  • Desiging original graphics and flowcharts
  • Editing copy for clarity and simplicity

The Strategic Plan is strictly confidential, so I can’t post images online here. But if you shoot me an email, I can send over samples!

The Strategic Plan had 3 sections: a Faculty Update, a KPI Update, and a Mid-Point Review.

The Faculty Update got a major facelift from the previous year. I reformatted the pages to more closely align with current UWaterloo documentation, with bolder images and cleaner copy sections. Before, the page was filled with paragraphs with a few images scattered throughout. After, the page had one large image across the edge, with paragraphs situated in 3 columns. It was received positively by the Creative Services department.

I based the KPI Update off of the previous year’s template, as it was the most optimal way to display so much data in a small space. Great research had gone into the template — I was confident it would work best.

The Mid-Point Review was a special double-sided insert specifically documenting the milestones hit so far, unique to that year. I had complete creative freedom with the document, ensuring it included all pertinent information and was visually appealing. The document was received very positively by the president, Feridun Hamdallahpur.

USABILITY TESTING

Problem: The IAP website was prepping for a switch to a new web template. The current site was text-heavy with broken links and other misinformation.

Solution: Execute a two-tiered usability test. First, scour the website for all errors, grammatical issues, and areas of improvement, ranking them in order of importance. Second, verify the severity of these issues with scenario-based user testing.

Details of the user testing can be found on the UX Research & Design page.

After completing these tests, I drafted a report walking through my process, the conclusions I drew from the data, and next steps. View the report below.

User Testing Summary Report

A breakdown of the testing performed, and the methodology behind the actionable conclusions.

The user testing was a success, spanning 10 participants over 1.5 months. I screen- and audio-recorded all the tests for my records, interviewing users and asking questions as they walked through the scenarios.

Since the website had multiple use cases to tend to, I ensured there was a balance of participants experienced and inexperienced with the website. This way, I had data from familiar users and outside perspectives.
